Females naturally carry more essential fat (~12%) due to sex-specific fat in breasts, pelvis, buttocks, and thighs — important for hormonal function and childbearing.

Key definitions
E
Essential fat — cannot lose this without compromising normal physiological function
Found in nerve tissues, bone marrow, organs, and all cellular membranes. Females have sex-specific fat (~5–9% extra) for breast tissue and hormonal (estrogen) production.
S
Storage fat — the energy reserve; what increases/decreases with diet and exercise
~3,500 kcal per pound. Can be subcutaneous (under skin) or visceral (around organs).
≠
Overweight ≠ overfat
A muscular athlete may be "overweight" by BMI but have low body fat. Overfat = % fat exceeds healthy range regardless of total weight.
Memory trick for female vs male essential fat: "Women need 12, Men need 3"
The 9% difference is sex-specific fat (breast ~4%, pelvis/buttocks/thigh) needed for estrogen production and childbearing. It is NOT optional.

Central vs peripheral fat deposition

Where your body stores fat is genetically determined — you cannot choose through exercise ("spot reduction" is a myth).

Central (android) — "Apple"
Fat deposited around abdomen & thorax

Subcutaneous (SAT) below skin
Visceral (VAT) around organs

High VAT = ↑ risk of heart disease, Type II diabetes, cardiometabolic syndrome
Peripheral (gynoid) — "Pear"
Fat deposited around buttocks, hips & upper thighs

Mostly subcutaneous fat

Lower health risk than central deposition — subcutaneous fat is less metabolically dangerous than visceral fat
Why visceral fat is more dangerous
!
Visceral fat is metabolically active — releases inflammatory cytokines and free fatty acids directly into the portal circulation
This directly stresses the liver, disrupts insulin signaling, and promotes arterial inflammation → higher cardiometabolic risk
!
Subcutaneous fat (peripheral) is less dangerous metabolically
It is still excess fat, but it doesn't dump inflammatory compounds directly into the liver via the portal vein
Spot reduction — why it doesn't work
✗
You cannot target fat loss to a specific body part through exercise
Fat cell atrophy follows a genetically programmed sequential order — "last on, first off." The last place you gained fat will be the first place you lose it, regardless of which muscles you exercise.
"Apple (central) = higher risk. Pear (peripheral) = lower risk."
Central fat may wrap around abdominal organs (visceral) — the dangerous kind. Peripheral fat stays mostly under skin (subcutaneous). You can't choose which shape you are — it's genetic.

Components of a healthy diet (USDA Dietary Reference Intake)

Nutrient-dense, calorie-poor. High complex carbs, low fat — especially low saturated and trans fat.

Carbohydrates — 45–65%

Mostly fruits, vegetables, whole grains. Avoid simple sugars and refined CHO.

Fat — 20–35%

Mostly unsaturated. Very little saturated (mostly animal products) or trans fat.

Protein — 10–35%

Lean sources preferred. ≤1.6 g/kg/day for strength gains.

Ultraprocessed foods (UPF) — avoid
~
~60% of the typical US diet comes from ultraprocessed foods
~70% of packaged foods in US are considered UPF. Most fast food, breakfast cereals, snack bars, frozen meals, packaged sweets.
!
UPF contain: high-fructose corn syrup, hydrogenated oils, protein isolates, chemical additives, artificial flavors, sweeteners, emulsifiers, preservatives
↑
UPF associated with: ↑ cardiometabolic syndrome, ↑ cognitive decline, ↑ depression & anxiety
Healthy diet = "45-65-20-35-10-35" → CHO · Fat · Protein ranges
CHO 45–65% (complex, whole foods) · Fat 20–35% (mostly unsaturated) · Protein 10–35% (lean sources). Nutrient dense, calorie poor. Minimize UPF.

Ineffective methods of fat loss

Crash dieting
1
Drastic caloric restriction → body detects starvation
Initial rapid weight loss is mostly water (from glycogen depletion) and some glucose — not fat
2
↓
BMR drops — body enters "starvation mode"
The body adapts by burning fewer calories to preserve itself. Less fat is actually lost than expected.
3
↓
Yo-yo dieting: repeated crash dieting → BMR stuck in starvation mode
Body chronically adjusts downward → progressively harder to lose fat with each cycle
High protein / low carb diets (Atkins, Zone)
✗
Nutrient poor — not an ideal long-term diet
✗
Starves cells of glucose → large water loss (from glycogen depletion) — not fat loss
✗
Short-term weight loss is mostly water, not adipose tissue
~
Protein is harder to digest → burns more kcal during digestion (thermic effect)
This is the one mechanism that works — but overall efficacy and long-term safety are controversial
Rubber/plastic/sauna suits
✗
Large body mass loss on each use — but almost zero fat loss
Weight lost is almost entirely water (sweating). It returns completely once properly rehydrated. No actual adipose tissue is lost.
Spot reduction
✗
You cannot target fat loss to a specific area by exercising that body part
Fat cell atrophy follows a genetically programmed sequential order. "Last on, first off." Doing crunches does not burn abdominal fat preferentially.
Fad diets
✗
If a diet works over a long period of time — it is not a fad
Most fad diets produce short-term weight loss through water and glycogen depletion, not sustained fat reduction.
Ineffective methods all share one thing: they don't create a sustainable caloric deficit from actual fat
Crash diets → water loss + starvation mode. Rubber suits → pure water loss. Spot reduction → myth. High protein → mostly water. Real fat loss requires a sustained caloric deficit over time.
Anorexia nervosa
Self-imposed starvation in an effort to lose weight and achieve thinness. Characterized by serious weight loss, refusal to eat, and a distorted perception of one's body weight/shape.
Severe caloric restriction → body breaks down lean mass and fat
Electrolyte imbalances → cardiac arrhythmias, organ failure
Highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder
Bulimia nervosa
Recurrent binge eating (consuming far more than normal in one sitting) followed by purging via self-induced vomiting, intense exercise, laxatives, or diuretics. Self-evaluation is unduly influenced by body shape and weight.
Erosion of tooth enamel from repeated vomiting (stomach acid)
Electrolyte imbalances → cardiac arrhythmias
Esophageal damage, GI complications
Psychological consequences: shame, secrecy, depression
Female athlete triad
Three interrelated disorders common in female athletes in body-image sports (gymnastics, dance, figure skating, distance running, wrestling weight classes).
1. Disordered eating → low energy availability, low calcium → body fat drops below essential (~12%)
2. Menstrual dysfunction / amenorrhea → estrogen drops when body fat is too low
3. Bone mineral loss → low calcium + low estrogen → premature osteoporosis, stress fractures
Male athlete triad
Three interrelated health problems in male athletes, particularly in weight-class and endurance sports.
1. Energy deficiency ("under-fueling") — chronic caloric restriction relative to training demands
2. Reproductive suppression — low testosterone, oligospermia (low sperm count), reduced libido
3. Poor bone health — stress fractures, reduced bone mineral density
Muscle dysmorphia
An altered self-image where someone believes their muscles are small and weak even though they are objectively large and strong. Often called "reverse anorexia."
Compulsive resistance training, excessive protein intake
Use of anabolic steroids and other ergogenic substances
Social isolation, anxiety, and depression related to missed workouts
Physical health risks from steroid use: liver damage, cardiovascular issues, hormonal disruption
Weight cutting (rapid weight loss for competition)
Practice of rapidly losing body mass over hours to days, common in weight-class sports (wrestling, MMA, boxing, judo, taekwondo). Three collegiate wrestlers died in 1998 within 2 months.
Methods: food restriction (83%), water loading (73%), sauna/sweat suits (60–70%), increased training

↓ Stroke volume → ↑ HR, ↓ thermoregulation
↓ Glycogen, ↓ testosterone, ↓ immune function, ↓ lean mass

Female triad: Disordered eating → Amenorrhea → Osteoporosis. Male triad: Under-fueling → Low testosterone/oligospermia → Poor bone health. Same root cause, different hormonal expressions.

The fat loss formula — Energy Stored = Energy Input ± Energy Output

All excess caloric intake — whether from carbohydrate, fat, OR protein — is stored in adipose tissue as storage fat. Fat loss requires consistently burning more calories than you consume.

Eat 3,000 kcal, burn 2,000 kcal → 1,000 kcal stored as fat
Eat 2,000 kcal, burn 3,000 kcal → 1,000 kcal removed from adipose tissue
Factor 1 — Caloric value of storage fat
~
1 lb of storage fat ≈ 3,500 kcal
This is the commonly cited figure. It assumes the energy density of pure fat (triglycerides). 96% of adipose tissue is made up of triglycerides.
!
BUT adipose tissue is NOT 100% pure fat
Adipose tissue composition: ~83% pure fat · ~15% water · ~2% protein. Therefore the true caloric equivalent of 1 lb of actual adipose tissue = 3,500 × 0.83 ≈ 2,995 kcal.
3,500 kcal = pure fat. 2,995 kcal = real adipose tissue (83% fat + 15% water + 2% protein)
The 2,995 figure is more biologically accurate for fat loss predictions in the real world.
Factor 2 — Fat cell behavior
↑
Fat gain = fat cell HYPERTROPHY (cells get bigger)
Storage adipose cells expand as triglycerides accumulate inside them
↓
Fat loss = fat cell ATROPHY (cells shrink)
Triglycerides are mobilized out of cells → cells shrink. The cells themselves do NOT disappear.
✗
No evidence of fat cell hyperplasia (new fat cells) in adults
Some hypothesize it may occur during infancy, puberty, and pregnancy — but not in general adult life.
Factor 3 — Exercise caloric expenditure
~
Walking OR jogging 1 mile ≈ 100 kcal
Moving your body mass 1 mile requires roughly the same total work regardless of pace. Running gets there faster but burns the same kcal per mile as walking.
5lb
To lose 5 lbs of fat through exercise alone = ~175 miles
5 lbs × 3,500 kcal ÷ 100 kcal/mile = 175 miles. At 5 miles/day = 35 days. This illustrates why diet + exercise together is far more practical than exercise alone.
Factor 4 — Fuel selection during exercise does NOT determine fat loss
✗
The "fat burning zone" myth — low intensity exercise does NOT produce more total fat loss than high intensity
Low intensity burns a HIGHER % of fat per minute, but you burn fewer total kcal. High intensity burns more kcal/min but uses mostly CHO. TOTAL caloric deficit over time is what determines fat loss — not which fuel was burned during the workout.
✓
Low intensity advantage: can be performed longer → possibly more total kcal per session
Especially useful for beginners, older adults, or those with orthopedic problems
✓
High intensity advantage: burns more kcal per minute → better for time-limited individuals
Factor 5 — Aerobic vs resistance training for fat loss
A
Aerobic training: typically burns more total kcal per session
More efficient for creating a caloric deficit during the workout itself
R
Resistance training: increases lean body mass → raises basal metabolic rate (BMR)
More muscle = more calories burned at rest 24/7. Compounds over time. Protects lean mass during a caloric deficit.
★
Ideal = both aerobic + resistance training combined
Aerobic burns more kcal per session. Resistance builds the metabolic engine that burns more kcal all day long.
Factor 6 — Safe rate of fat loss
↓
1–4 lbs of fat loss per week = safe and appropriate
Greater mass loss during the first few weeks is common but reflects water loss from glycogen depletion — not pure fat.
H₂O
Why early weight loss looks bigger than it is
Glycogen holds ~3g of water per gram. Depleting glycogen stores early in a diet → rapid water weight loss → scale drops fast → gives false impression of fat loss rate.
Summary: the 6 factors
1. 3,500 kcal = 1 lb pure fat · 2,995 kcal = 1 lb real adipose tissue (83% fat)
2. Fat cells shrink (atrophy) during fat loss — they don't disappear
3. Walking/jogging 1 mile ≈ 100 kcal regardless of pace
4. Fuel selection (fat vs CHO) doesn't determine fat loss — total caloric deficit does
5. Aerobic burns more kcal/session · Resistance raises BMR long-term
6. 1–4 lbs/week safe · Early rapid loss = water, not fat
